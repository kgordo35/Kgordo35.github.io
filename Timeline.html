The Creation of Cookies

The First web browser cookie was designed by Lou Montulli in 1994. Montulli at the time was employed at Netscape Communications which during the 1990's was one of the leading companies in web browser development. The cookie that Montulli designed was the shopping cart cookie. This cookie allowed users to add items to their online shopping cart while maintaing the items a user added to their shopping cart even if they were to navigate away from the website. One year later Montulli secured the patent for the cookie and was later added to Internet Explorer. Within two years of the creation of the first web browser cookie, third-party cookies were created. Once cookies became a staple element in web browsing users began to have concerns about how much information cookies were collecting and how that information was being used. The first article published on this was released in 1996 by the Financial Times. Follwing this article organizations such as the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) advised Netscape and Internet Explorer via the RFC 2109 to allow users to have substantial control over cookies. However neither Netscape nor Internet Explorer implemented these changes. As time went on more and more third-party cookies were being created for the use of tracking users behaviors and information for the use of advertising purposes. For a little over a decade there was little to no regulation on third-party cookies and how users could control them. In 2010 The Wall Street Journal published a story with an experiment that brought to light just how pervasive these third-party cookies were. The experiment conducted found that "50 sites checked by TWSJ have installed 3,000 tracking files on the computer used in the study"(Bober, 2023). That is an average of 60 files per site visited. A year after this article was published the FTC implemented a law that stated that users must be aware if a third-party cookie is active on their device.
